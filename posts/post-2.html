Post #2: Analysis of South Korean Immigration policies

Attitudes Towards Immigration

The current immigration rate in South Korea stands at 2.61 migrant(s) / 1,000 population.1 Until the 1990's Korea had been relatively isolated due to its geographical location on the Korean Peninsula. As a result, the nation had undeveloped views of multi-cultural interactions.2 With a generally homogenous society, issues of racism and discrimination become prevalent when other cultures were introduced. In the early 90's South Korea began accepting migrant workers in response to a weakening labor force, which was caused by declining birth rates and increasing labor costs. In 1995, the "Myeong-Dong Migrant Labor Protest" took place to expose inhumane working conditions placed on migrant workers.3 However, despite some improvement in attitudes towards immigrants, concerns about poor treatment of these migrant workers persists even into the present day.4

Refugees

Although South Korea was the first East Asian country to join the Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees, it is still notoriously difficult to gain refugee status. Since the year 1994, there has been a 6.7% rate of acceptance for refugees seeking asylum in South Korea.5 However, North Korean defectors are automatically granted South Korean citizenship.6

So What?

Because of the geography of the Korean Peninsula, intercultural interactions are a more recent phenomena. This is a common theme that is observable throughout the world as a result of globalization and was a topic of discussion in class. How do we navigate coexisting with various cultures in a way that promotes harmony and peace? These relationships are complex, and although communication is a vital aspect, it is not always an effective solution. Learning about each others cultures from reputable sources within those communities is a starting point to breaking down cultural barriers.
